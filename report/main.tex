\begin{abstract}
Music genre classification is a fundamental task in music information retrieval, enabling automated organization and recommendation of digital music libraries.
This paper presents a comparative study of traditional machine learning models---Support Vector Machine (SVM) and Random Forest---against a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for classifying audio signals into ten genres using the GTZAN dataset.
We extract hand-crafted audio features (MFCCs, spectral centroid, zero crossing rate) for the traditional models and train a four-block CNN on log-mel spectrograms derived from three-second audio segments.
To ensure a fair comparison, all models are evaluated on the same track-level test set using stratified splits that prevent data leakage between segments of the same track.
We further investigate feature discriminability through importance analysis and assess model robustness under varying levels of additive Gaussian noise.
Our results show that the CNN with majority voting achieves the highest test accuracy of 78.7\%, followed by the SVM at 72.0\% and Random Forest at 67.3\%. The CNN also demonstrates superior noise robustness, retaining 32.0\% accuracy at 5~dB SNR compared to 20.7\% for SVM and 24.0\% for Random Forest.
The feature importance analysis reveals that chroma STFT statistics, perceptual feature variance, and mid-range MFCCs are the most discriminative across both traditional models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The rapid growth of digital music databases and streaming platforms has made automated music genre classification (MGC) an essential task for content organization, recommendation systems, and music information retrieval (MIR)~\cite{fu2011survey}. Manual tagging of musical genres is inherently subjective, labor-intensive, and incapable of scaling to the thousands of new tracks uploaded daily. Automating this process through machine learning enables platforms to organize libraries efficiently and deliver personalized listening experiences.

The GTZAN dataset~\cite{tzanetakis2002musical}, often referred to as the ``MNIST of sounds,'' has served as a standard benchmark for MGC research for over two decades. Despite its relatively small size (1,000 tracks across 10 genres), it continues to be widely used for comparative studies due to its accessibility and well-understood characteristics~\cite{sturm2013gtzan}.

A central question in audio classification is whether traditional feature-engineered models can match or exceed the performance of deep learning architectures on moderately-sized datasets. While CNNs have demonstrated remarkable success in large-scale audio tasks, the limited size of GTZAN may favor models with stronger inductive biases derived from domain-specific features.

This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A rigorous comparative study between SVM, Random Forest, and CNN classifiers, with track-level data splitting to prevent leakage from augmented segments.
    \item A feature importance analysis identifying which audio descriptors contribute most significantly to genre discrimination.
    \item A noise robustness evaluation measuring how each model degrades under synthetic additive noise at multiple signal-to-noise ratios.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

Tzanetakis and Cook~\cite{tzanetakis2002musical} introduced the GTZAN dataset and proposed the use of timbral features, rhythmic content, and pitch-based features for genre classification, achieving approximately 61\% accuracy with Gaussian mixture models. Subsequent work has explored a wide range of approaches.

\textbf{Traditional ML approaches.} Support Vector Machines have been extensively applied to MGC, leveraging features such as Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs)~\cite{logan2000mel}, spectral centroid, spectral rolloff, and zero crossing rate. Li et al.~\cite{li2010automatic} demonstrated that ensemble methods combining multiple feature sets can improve classification accuracy. Random Forests~\cite{breiman2001random} offer natural feature importance rankings and have been shown to perform competitively on audio classification tasks.

\textbf{Deep learning approaches.} Costa et al.~\cite{costa2017evaluation} evaluated CNNs on mel-spectrogram representations, treating the audio classification problem as an image recognition task. Choi et al.~\cite{choi2017convolutional} introduced convolutional recurrent architectures that capture both local spectral patterns and temporal dynamics. Dieleman and Schrauwen~\cite{dieleman2014end} explored end-to-end learning directly from raw waveforms, bypassing explicit feature extraction.

\textbf{Data augmentation for audio.} Park et al.~\cite{park2019specaugment} proposed SpecAugment, a simple augmentation method that applies frequency and time masking to spectrograms, significantly improving robustness in speech recognition. We adopt a similar strategy for our CNN training.

\textbf{GTZAN limitations.} Sturm~\cite{sturm2013gtzan} documented several faults in the GTZAN dataset, including mislabeled tracks and a truncated file (\texttt{jazz.00054.wav}). Our pipeline explicitly handles these known issues through validation and exclusion.

\section{Dataset and Preprocessing}

\subsection{GTZAN Dataset}
The GTZAN dataset contains 1,000 audio tracks, each 30 seconds long, evenly distributed across 10 genres: blues, classical, country, disco, hip-hop, jazz, metal, pop, reggae, and rock. All files are mono WAV format sampled at 22,050~Hz with 16-bit depth.

\subsection{Data Validation}
We perform automated validation of all audio files, checking for duration consistency and loading integrity. One known corrupted file (\texttt{jazz.00054.wav}, which is truncated to approximately 91\% of normal length) is excluded, leaving 999 valid tracks.

\subsection{Data Augmentation}
Each 30-second track is segmented into ten non-overlapping 3-second clips, expanding the effective dataset from 999 to approximately 9,990 samples. This augmentation provides sufficient training data for the CNN while maintaining manageable computational requirements.

\subsection{Train/Validation/Test Split}
To prevent data leakage, all splits are performed at the \textbf{track level} before segmentation. We use a 70/15/15 stratified split, ensuring equal genre proportions across partitions. Critically, all 10 segments from a single 30-second track are assigned to the same partition, preventing the model from learning to recognize track-specific characteristics rather than genre-level patterns.

All features are standardized using a \texttt{StandardScaler} fitted exclusively on the training set to prevent information leakage from validation or test statistics.

For the CNN, we compute log-mel spectrograms from each 3-second segment using 128 mel frequency bands, an FFT window of 2,048 samples, and a hop length of 512 samples. The resulting spectrogram has dimensions $128 \times 130$ (frequency bins $\times$ time frames) and is normalized to $[0, 1]$.

\subsection{Traditional Models}

\textbf{Support Vector Machine (SVM).} We train an SVM classifier~\cite{cortes1995support} with hyperparameter optimization via 5-fold cross-validated grid search over $C \in \{0.1, 1, 10, 100\}$, kernel $\in \{\text{RBF}, \text{linear}\}$, and $\gamma \in \{\text{scale}, \text{auto}\}$. The scoring metric is macro-averaged F1-score.

\textbf{Random Forest (RF).} We train a Random Forest classifier~\cite{breiman2001random} with grid search over $n_{\text{estimators}} \in \{100, 200, 500\}$, $\text{max\_depth} \in \{10, 20, 30, \text{None}\}$, and $\text{min\_samples\_split} \in \{2, 5\}$.

\subsection{Convolutional Neural Network}

We design a four-block CNN architecture (Figure~\ref{fig:cnn_arch}) that processes single-channel mel-spectrograms of shape $128 \times 130 \times 1$.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\fbox{\parbox{0.9\linewidth}{\small
\textbf{Input:} $128 \times 130 \times 1$ \\[4pt]
$\rightarrow$ Conv2D(32, 3$\times$3) $\rightarrow$ BN $\rightarrow$ ReLU $\rightarrow$ MaxPool(2$\times$2) $\rightarrow$ Dropout(0.3) \\
$\rightarrow$ Conv2D(64, 3$\times$3) $\rightarrow$ BN $\rightarrow$ ReLU $\rightarrow$ MaxPool(2$\times$2) $\rightarrow$ Dropout(0.3) \\
$\rightarrow$ Conv2D(128, 3$\times$3) $\rightarrow$ BN $\rightarrow$ ReLU $\rightarrow$ MaxPool(2$\times$2) $\rightarrow$ Dropout(0.3) \\
$\rightarrow$ Conv2D(256, 3$\times$3) $\rightarrow$ BN $\rightarrow$ ReLU $\rightarrow$ MaxPool(2$\times$2) $\rightarrow$ Dropout(0.3) \\[4pt]
$\rightarrow$ GlobalAvgPool $\rightarrow$ Dense(256) $\rightarrow$ BN $\rightarrow$ ReLU $\rightarrow$ Dropout(0.5) \\
$\rightarrow$ Dense(10, softmax) \\[4pt]
\textbf{Output:} 10-class probability distribution
}}
\caption{CNN architecture for mel-spectrogram classification.}
\label{fig:cnn_arch}
\end{figure}

Key design choices include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Batch Normalization}~\cite{ioffe2015batch} after each convolutional layer to stabilize training and enable higher learning rates.
    \item \textbf{Dropout}~\cite{srivastava2014dropout} at 0.3 after pooling layers and 0.5 before the final classification layer, combined with L2 regularization ($\lambda = 0.001$) on all kernel weights.
    \item \textbf{Global Average Pooling} instead of flattening, which reduces the parameter count and acts as structural regularization.
\end{itemize}

The model is trained using the Adam optimizer~\cite{kingma2014adam} with an initial learning rate of $10^{-3}$, \texttt{ReduceLROnPlateau} (factor 0.5, patience 5), and early stopping (patience 10, restoring best weights). We apply SpecAugment-style augmentation~\cite{park2019specaugment} during training, including frequency masking and time masking.

For track-level evaluation, the CNN classifies each of the 10 segments from a 30-second track independently, and the final prediction is determined by majority voting. This ensures a fair comparison with the traditional models, which operate on full 30-second feature vectors.

\subsection{Noise Robustness Protocol}

To assess model robustness, we inject additive white Gaussian noise into the test set audio at signal-to-noise ratios (SNR) of 40, 30, 20, 10, and 5~dB. For each noise level, we re-extract features (for SVM/RF) and regenerate mel-spectrograms (for CNN), then evaluate all models on the degraded data. This protocol directly answers our third research question regarding noise sensitivity.

\section{Experiments and Results}

All experiments are conducted using Python with scikit-learn~\cite{pedregosa2011scikit} for traditional ML and TensorFlow~\cite{tensorflow2015} for deep learning. Audio processing uses librosa~\cite{mcfee2015librosa}. The random seed is fixed at 42 for reproducibility.

\subsection{Model Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:comparison} presents the classification performance of all three models on the held-out test set. The CNN with track-level majority voting achieves the highest accuracy at 78.7\%, outperforming the SVM (72.0\%) by 6.7 percentage points and the Random Forest (67.3\%) by 11.3 percentage points. The SVM selected $C = 100$ with an RBF kernel and $\gamma = \text{scale}$ through grid search, while the Random Forest selected 100 estimators with no maximum depth and a minimum samples split of 5.

Examining per-class performance, the CNN achieves particularly strong results on hip-hop (F1: 0.91), metal (F1: 0.93), and jazz (F1: 0.89), while struggling most with country (recall: 0.40) and rock (F1: 0.56). The SVM performs best on hip-hop (F1: 0.85) and classical (F1: 0.87) but has difficulty distinguishing blues (F1: 0.52) and reggae (F1: 0.57). The Random Forest shows similar patterns, with its weakest performance on rock (F1: 0.31) and disco (F1: 0.47).

Figure~\ref{fig:training_curves} shows the training and validation loss and accuracy curves over the course of CNN training. \subsection{Feature Importance Analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:fi_rf} presents the top 20 features ranked by Gini importance from the Random Forest classifier. The most discriminative feature is \texttt{chroma\_stft\_mean} (importance: 0.052), followed by \texttt{perceptr\_var} (0.045) and \texttt{chroma\_stft\_var} (0.038). Permutation importance analysis on the SVM reveals a different ranking, with \texttt{mfcc4\_mean} (0.052) and \texttt{mfcc11\_mean} (0.036) leading, though both models agree on the importance of \texttt{perceptr\_var} and \texttt{chroma\_stft\_var}. The consensus set of features appearing in both models' top~10 includes chroma STFT statistics, perceptual feature variance, several mid-range MFCC coefficients (4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 17), and zero crossing rate. This suggests that timbral characteristics captured by MFCCs and chroma features are the primary drivers of genre discrimination.

\subsection{Noise Robustness}

Figure~\ref{fig:noise} illustrates how each model's accuracy degrades under additive Gaussian noise at five SNR levels. At 40~dB (minimal noise), all models retain near-clean performance: CNN at 78.7\%, SVM at 70.0\%, and Random Forest at 67.3\%. At 20~dB, a moderate noise level, the CNN retains 52.0\% accuracy compared to 40.0\% for SVM and 39.3\% for Random Forest. Under severe noise at 5~dB, the CNN still achieves 32.0\% accuracy (well above the 10\% random baseline), while SVM drops to 20.7\% and Random Forest to 24.0\%. The CNN's superior noise resilience likely stems from its learned spectral representations, which can capture robust patterns in mel-spectrograms even when noise corrupts individual frequency bands.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{Genre confusion patterns.} The confusion matrices reveal that rock is consistently the most difficult genre across all three models, frequently confused with country, blues, and disco. This reflects the broad stylistic diversity within rock music and its genuine overlap with neighboring genres. Conversely, classical and metal are classified with high precision by all models, likely due to their distinctive spectral characteristics---classical music features prominently harmonic tonal content, while metal exhibits high-energy broadband spectral profiles. The CNN's low recall on country (40\%) suggests that country tracks are being distributed across multiple other genre predictions, a pattern not as pronounced in the traditional models.

\textbf{Traditional ML vs. CNN.} Despite the limited size of GTZAN (999 tracks), the CNN outperforms both traditional models by a meaningful margin (78.7\% vs.\ 72.0\% for SVM and 67.3\% for RF). This 6.7 percentage point advantage over SVM demonstrates that even on small datasets, the CNN's ability to learn hierarchical spectral representations from mel-spectrograms provides an advantage over hand-crafted feature vectors. The train--validation gap (~13\%) suggests the CNN has not fully converged to a generalizable solution, and additional data or transfer learning could further improve performance.

\textbf{Feature discriminability.} The feature importance analysis reveals an interesting divergence between the two traditional models: the Random Forest prioritizes global spectral descriptors (chroma STFT, spectral bandwidth), while the SVM relies more heavily on mid-range MFCCs (coefficients 4, 5, 11). Both models agree on the importance of perceptual feature variance and chroma STFT variance, suggesting these capture complementary aspects of timbral and harmonic content that are broadly useful for genre discrimination.

\textbf{Noise resilience.} The noise robustness experiment confirms that the CNN degrades more gracefully than both traditional models under increasing noise. At 20~dB SNR, the CNN retains 52.0\% accuracy while SVM and RF drop to approximately 40\%. At the harshest condition (5~dB), the CNN still achieves 32.0\%, over three times the 10\% random baseline. This resilience is likely due to the CNN's ability to exploit spatial patterns in spectrograms that remain partially intact even under noise corruption, whereas the hand-crafted features (particularly spectral statistics) are more directly impacted by additive noise.

\textbf{Limitations.} This study is limited by the relatively small GTZAN dataset, which may not capture the full diversity within each genre. Additionally, the single-label classification paradigm does not account for tracks that genuinely span multiple genres. The CPU-only training environment constrained our ability to explore larger architectures or more extensive hyperparameter searches. Future work could explore transfer learning from larger audio datasets and multi-label classification approaches.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presents a comparative study of music genre classification approaches on the GTZAN dataset, evaluating SVM, Random Forest, and CNN classifiers. We implemented a rigorous experimental pipeline with track-level data splitting, comprehensive feature extraction, and a four-block CNN architecture trained on log-mel spectrograms with SpecAugment-style augmentation.

Our key findings include: (1) the CNN with majority voting achieves the highest test accuracy of 78.7\%, outperforming SVM (72.0\%) and Random Forest (67.3\%); (2) chroma STFT statistics, perceptual feature variance, and mid-range MFCCs are the most discriminative features, with notable divergence between Gini and permutation importance rankings; and (3) the CNN demonstrates superior noise robustness, retaining 32.0\% accuracy at 5~dB SNR compared to 20.7\% for SVM. These results suggest that CNNs can outperform well-tuned traditional classifiers even on small datasets like GTZAN, and that their learned representations provide greater resilience to real-world noise degradation.

Future directions include exploring transfer learning with pre-trained audio models, evaluating on larger and more diverse datasets, and extending the system to multi-label genre classification.
